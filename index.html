Definition of Authentic Community Engagement
"

Authentic Community Engagement is a genuine, inclusive, and ongoing partnership between school leaders and community members that values diverse voices and lived experiences. It ensures accessible, culturally responsive communication and removes barriers to participation. Engagement involves broad stakeholder involvement — especially early in the process — with meaningful two-way communication, transparency, and accountability. It is deliberate about engaging a sufficiently broad and representative group of stakeholders to ensure that no single political, institutional, or special interest group is able to dominate or steer the process toward its own agenda.

Decision makers actively listen, act on feedback, and translate community input into real influence over decisions — not just acknowledgment. They share clear outcomes, maintaining trust through follow-through and public accountability. Engagement is sustained beyond crises or elections, driven by community priorities, and creates safe spaces where all feel welcomed and empowered. Ultimately, it fosters shared decision-making and collective action to support student success and community well-being.

Coalition for Authentic Community Engagement — Adopted March 2026

This definition was developed through a community-led input process conducted by the Coalition for Authentic Community Engagement (CACE) in partnership with Kids First Chicago. It reflects direct community voice from 29 respondents — 16 English handwritten surveys, 4 Spanish handwritten surveys, and 9 digital responses — collected from educators, parents, community members, and advocates across Chicago.

It was adopted in March 2026 in response to a sustained pattern of CPS decisions made without adequate community involvement. This is not aspirational language — it is an accountability standard the Coalition holds the Board and district to in any engagement process. Read more on the About page →

A Process Framework for Authentic Engagement

Authentic community engagement is not a single event or a checkbox. It is a disciplined process that requires preparation, honesty, and a genuine willingness to be changed by what you hear.

1
Before you begin

Determine the level of decision-making power the community should hold.

Before any engagement begins, CPS must reference the Spectrum of Inclusive Partnerships and make an explicit determination: how much influence will the community have over this decision? Some decisions rest with the administration or the Board. Others require meaningful input. And some require genuine deference, placing the outcome in the hands of those who will live with it. This determination must be made transparently, before engagement begins — not after.

2

Match the scale and reach of engagement to the decision.

Once the level of community power is established, the scope of engagement must reflect it. Authentic engagement is not complete until the target stakeholders have been meaningfully reached. For major decisions, that may mean thousands of people; for local ones, several hundred. Multiple modalities should be used to maximize diversity and reach, ensuring that participation is not limited to those who are easiest to access.

3

Ensure the community is making an informed decision, not just a reactive one.

Authentic engagement requires that community members have sufficient knowledge about the consequences of the options before them. Asking someone what they want and simply acting on it is not engagement — and can be a form of coercion. But asking someone what they want while withholding or limiting information is equally harmful. Genuine engagement takes time and intentionality. The community deserves both the question and the full picture.

4

Resist unnecessary urgency.

Decisions made quickly are often made in service of political positioning or the appearance of progress, not in service of the people most affected. When engagement is rushed, the resulting decisions tend to be fragile: they do not reflect broad consensus, they do not hold, and they get reversed when the next administration takes power without doing the same work. The durability of a decision is a direct measure of how authentically it was reached. Do the work. When you have achieved the required scale and depth of engagement, the direction forward becomes clear — and it sticks.

5

Never begin an engagement process when the outcome has already been decided.

Authentic engagement is a trust fall for decision makers. But the evidence is clear: when communities are given good information and genuine power, they make decisions rooted in the well-being of their children and their neighbors. Trusting the community to decide for itself is not a risk — it is the point. For a district with Chicago's history, it is also an act of anti-racism: a deliberate relinquishment of power toward those it has too often been withheld from.

Follow this process and the outcomes will speak for themselves. Decisions reached through authentic engagement carry broad support because they come from the stakeholders themselves. They endure because the people living with them helped build them.

Stakeholder Inclusion & Engagement Standards

These standards define who must be included in engagement, at what scale, and with what level of influence — based on the scope and nature of the decision.

The Spectrum of Inclusive Partnerships

Before determining who to engage and at what scale, CPS must first determine how the community will be engaged and what level of influence they will have. The CPS Equity Framework's Spectrum of Inclusive Partnerships provides this foundation. It is CPS's responsibility to identify where a given decision sits on this spectrum before engaging the community. This declaration is part of the engagement process itself — not an afterthought.

Listen Inform Consult Involve Collaborate Empower
Goal Provide stakeholders space and time to be heard. Provide balanced information to assist understanding of the problem and options. Obtain stakeholder feedback on analysis, alternatives, and decisions. Work directly with stakeholders to ensure concerns and aspirations are consistently understood. Partner with stakeholders in each aspect of the decision, including developing solutions. Place final decision-making in the hands of the stakeholders.
Promise We will listen to your concerns with the intention of understanding. We will keep you informed. We will listen, acknowledge your concerns, and provide feedback on how input influenced the decision. We will work with you to ensure your concerns are directly reflected in the alternatives developed. We will formulate and prioritize solutions together and incorporate your advice to the maximum extent possible. We will implement what you decide.

About the Coalition for Authentic Community Engagement

In response to recent actions by the Chicago Board of Education, over two dozen Chicago community and education organizations have united to demand genuine community involvement in CPS policymaking.

Why We Formed

The Coalition for Authentic Community Engagement was formed in response to a sustained pattern of major decisions made by the Chicago Board of Education and CPS district leadership without adequate involvement of the communities most affected by those decisions.

Our member organizations — representing parents, students, educators, faith communities, immigrant communities, and neighborhood advocates across Chicago — share a common conviction: that authentic engagement is not a courtesy. It is a requirement for decisions to be durable, equitable, and legitimate.

The CACE Framework was developed through a community input process conducted in partnership with Kids First Chicago. It reflects direct community voice from 29 respondents — 16 English handwritten surveys, 4 Spanish handwritten surveys, and 9 digital responses — collected from educators, parents, community members, and advocates across Chicago. The definition, process framework, and stakeholder standards are an accountability tool — a standard the Coalition holds CPS to in any engagement process.
